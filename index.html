Understanding Diabetes

Diabetes affects blood sugar regulation. The sections below explain it in simple language with credible sources.

What is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a chronic disease that affects how the body turns food into energy. Food is broken down into glucose, which enters the bloodstream. Insulin helps move glucose from the blood into body cells for energy.

In diabetes, the body either does not make enough insulin or cannot use insulin properly. This causes high blood sugar levels. Over time, high blood sugar can damage the heart, blood vessels, eyes, kidneys, and nerves.

Source: World Health Organization – Diabetes fact sheet

🩸

Types of Diabetes

  • Type 1 diabetes: The immune system attacks insulin-producing cells, so the body makes little or no insulin. People with Type 1 diabetes need insulin every day.
  • Type 2 diabetes: The body becomes resistant to insulin or does not make enough insulin. This is the most common type of diabetes.
  • Gestational diabetes: Diabetes that develops during pregnancy and can increase future risk of Type 2 diabetes.

Source: NIDDK – What Is Diabetes?

🧬

Common Symptoms

  • Frequent urination
  • Increased thirst or hunger
  • Feeling very tired
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow healing wounds
  • Unexplained weight loss

Symptoms may appear slowly, especially with Type 2 diabetes. Some people may not notice symptoms at first, which is why regular screening matters.

Source: CDC – Diabetes symptoms

😓

Causes and Risk Factors

The causes depend on the type of diabetes. Type 1 diabetes is linked to an autoimmune reaction. Type 2 diabetes is strongly linked to insulin resistance and can be influenced by lifestyle and family history.

  • Family history of diabetes
  • Being overweight or obese
  • Physical inactivity
  • Age and previous gestational diabetes

Source: CDC – Diabetes risk factors

Medication Guide

Medication should always be taken as directed by a healthcare professional.

How to Take Your Medication Safely

  1. Take your medication at the same time each day if your doctor recommends it.
  2. Follow the dose and instructions given by your healthcare provider.
  3. Do not skip doses, double doses, or stop medication without medical advice.
  4. Ask a doctor or pharmacist what to do if you miss a dose.
  5. Store medication correctly and check expiry dates.

Source: CDC – Diabetes treatment

✅ Do

  • Take medication on time.
  • Keep regular clinic follow-ups.
  • Ask about side effects.
  • Check blood sugar as advised.

❌ Don’t

  • Do not share medication.
  • Do not change dose alone.
  • Do not ignore severe side effects.
  • Do not stop treatment because you feel better.

Possible Side Effects

Some medicines can cause nausea, dizziness, low blood sugar, or stomach upset. Seek medical help if side effects are severe or persistent.

Stay Consistent, Stay Healthy

No daily medication checklist here — just practical adherence tips.

Helpful Tips

  • ⏰
    Set daily reminders.

    Use phone alarms or calendar reminders to reduce forgetfulness.

  • 📍
    Keep medication visible.

    Place it somewhere safe but easy to remember, away from children.

  • 🍽️
    Link medicine to a routine.

    Take medicine with a regular habit, such as breakfast, if allowed by your doctor.

  • 🧳
    Plan when travelling.

    Carry enough medication and keep prescriptions or clinic notes when needed.

  • 📈
    Track your progress.

    Record blood sugar, symptoms, and questions for your next clinic visit.

Source: CDC – Living with Diabetes

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I miss a dose?

Do not double your dose unless your healthcare provider tells you to. Ask your doctor or pharmacist for instructions that match your medication.

Source: CDC – Treatment

Can I stop my medication if I feel better?

No. Feeling better does not always mean diabetes is gone. Stopping medication without advice can cause blood sugar to rise again.

What foods should I avoid?

Focus on a balanced eating plan. Limit sugary drinks, highly processed foods, and excessive refined carbohydrates. A dietitian or healthcare worker can help personalize your plan.

Source: CDC – Healthy Eating

How often should I check my blood sugar?

This depends on your treatment plan. People using insulin may need to check more often. Follow instructions from your clinic.

Where can I find more support?

Use trusted organizations such as WHO, CDC, IDF, and your local health clinic.
